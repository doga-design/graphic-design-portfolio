FERN & SONS CYCLE CO.

Community-rooted bicycle workshop brand identity

Brand Identity2024 · Illustrator, InDesign, Photoshop
Scroll to explore
01

Branding a Three-Generation Workshop

How do you create an identity for a 75-year-old family bicycle shop that feels rooted without feeling stuck in the past?

Fern & Sons Cycle Co. is a third-generation bicycle workshop in Toronto's Annex neighbourhood. For over 75 years, they've focused on honest repair, skilled hands, and long-term relationships with local riders.

This was a school project to design a complete brand identity for a small, community-based business. The challenge was to reflect the shop's legacy while making it feel approachable and relevant to today's riders—not precious, not corporate, just real.

I explored three visual directions: Swiss precision (too clinical), Bauhaus energy (too playful), and organic craft (just right). The final direction combined Art Nouveau-inspired flowing lines with structured clarity—rooted but not rustic, elegant but not fussy.

Timeline

3 weeks (concept through campaign)

Deliverables

Brand identity system, stationery, poster campaign, website mockups, branded merchandise

Positioning

The Annex's community-focused bicycle workshop—providing careful, reliable repair for riders who depend on honest service and familiar hands

02

Movement and Heritage

The wordmark uses Awesome Serif—a display face with character and warmth. The chevron icon is modular, referencing bicycle chain links and forward motion. The fern-inspired pattern wraps around the arrow, creating texture without clutter.

The lockup is flexible enough to work on everything from business cards to bike tags. It feels handmade but not fussy, confident but not corporate.

The palette—evergreen, antique white, and night bordeaux—avoids bright sporty colors in favor of calm, trustworthy tones. These aren't trend colors. They're the colors of a shop that's been there for decades.

view brand website
03

Community Ride Posters

The shop hosts weekly community rides every Sunday at 9AM. The poster campaign became the brand's public face.

Three poster variations use the same layout with different color treatments: yellow, green, and red. Each features bold type, grayscale photography, and the fern-chevron pattern as a graphic device. The copy is simple: "Community Ride | Sunday | 9AM."

The posters work at distance (bold color blocks, large type) and up close (pattern details, brand signature). Designed for bus shelters, shop windows, and community boards—wherever riders might see them.

04

System in Action

The identity needed to work across stationery, digital, print, and merchandise—every touchpoint where riders interact with the shop.

Each application uses the same core elements—logo, patterns, color palette, typography—but adapts to context. Business cards reveal the pattern on the back. Bike tags use simple messages like "Your bike is ready. Shaped with care by our hands." The website balances warmth with usability.

05

A Brand That Feels Like Home

An identity that honors 75 years of skilled work without feeling stuck in the past.

The final system is warm, trustworthy, and flexible. It positions Fern & Sons as rooted in community and craft—approachable but never casual, skilled but never intimidating. The kind of shop you'd bring your bike to for decades.

Highlights

- Rooted visual language that feels local, not corporate
- Flexible system that works across all touchpoints
- Campaign-ready with posters and branded merchandise
- Approachable premium positioning

ADOBE EXPRESS

Integrated campaign to shift Gen Z perception

Campaign Strategy2024 · Advertising I Course Project
Scroll to explore
01

Making Adobe Feel Like Theirs

How do you convince a generation that sees Adobe as "too professional" that it's actually built for the way they create?

Adobe is iconic. But Gen Z doesn't see it that way. To them, Adobe feels too complex, too slow, too professional—not for people like them. Meanwhile, Canva and Figma feel instant, cultural, made for them.

This was a school advertising project to reposition Adobe Express for Gen Z creators. The challenge: shift perception without changing the product. Show that Adobe isn't intimidating—it's already built for the way Gen Z creates: fast, experimental, playful.

Timeline

School project (Advertising I)

Deliverables

Campaign strategy, digital ads, OOH, experiential activation

Target

18–26-year-old creatives already making things, mostly with free tools

02

From Toys to Tools

Gen Z grew up building and experimenting with physical toys. That instinct never disappeared—they just create the same way digitally now.

The insight: Gen Z creates nonstop using fast, mobile-first tools. Adobe Express fits this naturally. The campaign bridges past and present: "What you built then, you can build now. Just with different tools."

The shift needed: BEFORE—"Adobe is for professionals, not for me." AFTER—"Adobe fits the way I create. I'm legit, finally using Adobe tools." Strategy: show Adobe is approachable. Behavior: sign up for Express, start using Photoshop to refine.

03

Remember Building?

Instagram carousels use nostalgic visuals—sticker sheets and building blocks. Copy: "Remember sticker-sheets? Collecting, arranging, placing?" → "Well, you still do all that. Just with different tools. Adobe."

OOH and transit campaigns show Adobe Express UI overlaid on childhood creative activities. "What you drew then, you can build now." Bus wraps, billboards, transit posters—all with "Build with Adobe" CTAs.

04

The Playout Wall

An interactive installation that turns physical play into finished digital posters—powered by Adobe.

Playout Wall is a physical grid where people place modular blocks to build layouts. A camera captures it, translates it into a digital poster in Adobe Express and Photoshop. You play on the wall, Adobe turns it into a real poster.

CTRLBREAK

Exhibition identity for a Neville Brody retrospective

BrandingIllustrator · Figma · InDesign
Scroll to explore
01

Breaking the rules, on purpose.

How do you design an identity for a designer who spent his career dismantling everyone else's rules?

Neville Brody rewrote typography in the 1980s. As art director of The Face, he fractured letterforms, ignored grids, treated type as image. His work was aggressive, political, unapologetic.

The challenge wasn't to mimic his work—that would be lazy. It was to channel his ethos: breaking conventions requires understanding them first. CTRL+BREAK became the answer. A keyboard command that interrupts a running process.

Timeline

3 weeks

Deliverables

Logo system, exhibition graphics, brand guidelines

Context

School project — pitchbook stage

02

Influence & Intent

Influence

Controlled Chaos
Brody's layouts felt chaotic but were meticulously controlled. Every violation was intentional. I wanted CTRL+BREAK to work the same way.

The Grid

Structure Built to Break
Built a rigid modular grid, then designed the identity to deliberately violate it. Logos shift. Type runs off edges. But every break follows logic.

Typography

Punk Meets Precision
Industrial typography, hard angles, stencil cuts—visual languages from protest posters. Raw energy with technical rigor.

Overview

This was a school project to design a complete exhibition identity and pitchbook. The pitchbook is a visual proposal showing how the identity would work across posters, signage, merchandise, and gallery spaces—designed to pitch the retrospective to museums and cultural institutions.
